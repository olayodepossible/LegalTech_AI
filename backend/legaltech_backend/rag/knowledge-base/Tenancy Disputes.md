## Abstract

This paper examined tenancy disputes within the context of Nigerian property law, highlighting the statutory, common law, and policy dimensions that define the relationship between landlords and tenants. The study focused on the interpretation and enforcement of tenancy rights as regulated by the Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Acts and similar tenancy laws enacted by various states. It was established that disputes often arose from failure to pay rent, unlawful evictions, non-compliance with quit notice requirements, breach of tenancy terms, and lack of understanding of legal obligations. The paper also reviewed types of tenancy recognized under Nigerian law, including weekly, monthly, yearly, and tenancies at will, while outlining the legal duties of both landlords and tenants. The analysis revealed that judicial decisions had played a critical role in shaping tenancy law, with landmark cases such as *Ihenacho v. Uzochukwu* and *Ayinke Stores Ltd v. Adebogun* offering important interpretive guidance. However, inconsistencies in court reasoning, jurisdictional overlap between magistrate and high courts, and procedural bottlenecks were identified as key enforcement challenges. The paper discussed available legal remedies such as specific performance, injunctions, statutory recovery processes, and the growing relevance of Alternative Dispute Resolution mechanisms like mediation and arbitration. Policy and legal implications were examined, including the fragmentation of tenancy laws, limited tenant protection, and lack of legal awareness among the public. The paper concluded by advocating for harmonization of laws, improved enforcement mechanisms, legal aid accessibility, and balanced regulation to protect both landlords and tenants.

**Keywords:** Tenancy disputes, landlord-tenant law, eviction enforcement, property rights

## Introduction

Tenancy arrangements have become a dominant feature of Nigeria's urban housing landscape due to the rising cost of land acquisition and limited access to mortgage financing. In many cities and towns across the country, individuals and businesses alike depend on rental housing for residential and commercial purposes. Tenancy agreements, whether written or oral, form the legal basis of this landlord–tenant relationship. These agreements outline the rights and obligations of both parties, including rent payment, duration, termination procedures, and notice periods. The legal framework regulating tenancy relationships in Nigeria derives from both common law and statutory provisions, such as the Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Act and various Tenancy Laws enacted by state legislatures. However, as observed by Igbokwe and Adeola, the implementation of these laws remains inconsistent, often leading to misunderstanding and exploitation in tenancy transactions.¹ The prevalence of tenancy disputes in Nigeria reflects broader socio-economic and institutional challenges. In most states, disputes frequently arise over rent increases, unlawful eviction, failure to serve quit notices, disagreements on lease renewal terms, and refusal to vacate premises after tenancy expiration. The growing rate of urban poverty, high unemployment, and housing shortages have exacerbated these issues, pushing landlords and tenants into conflict. According to Okorie and Nwankwo, a common trend is for landlords to resort to self-help measures such as forceful ejection, padlocking of doors, or disconnection of electricity and water supply, in contravention of established legal procedures.² Conversely, tenants have also been known to manipulate weak enforcement mechanisms to delay eviction or default on rent, creating financial losses and frustration for property owners. Courts, particularly magistrate courts, are overwhelmed with tenancy-related matters, many of which are protracted due to procedural delays and limited judicial capacity. These realities have raised questions about the effectiveness of existing legal remedies and the capacity of the legal system to protect the rights of both landlords and tenants.

The significance of this study lies in its focus on the judicial treatment of tenancy disputes and the legal remedies available under Nigerian property law. While several legislative provisions exist to regulate tenancies, their practical application remains riddled with inconsistencies, ambiguities, and enforcement difficulties. A detailed review of judicial decisions provides critical insight into how courts interpret tenancy laws and balance competing interests. As noted by Nnamani, the adjudication of tenancy disputes often reveals systemic lapses in Nigeria's legal system, including inadequate legal awareness, poor documentation, judicial delays, and occasional disregard for due process.³ By examining key cases and enforcement patterns, this paper aims to expose the gaps between legal theory and practice. It also aims to assess whether the remedies currently available are sufficient to resolve disputes effectively and restore order in landlord–tenant relationships. In doing so, the paper contributes to ongoing discussions on legal reform, tenant protection, and housing justice in Nigeria's evolving property law regime.

## Conceptual Framework

Tenancy as a legal concept forms the cornerstone of landlord-tenant relationships and is regulated by statutory and common law principles in Nigeria. A clear understanding of the term "tenancy" and its various types is essential to contextualize the legal disputes that often arise in rental agreements and occupancy matters. Tenancy is generally understood to refer to the possession or occupancy of land or property by a person (the tenant) under an agreement with the legal owner (the landlord), usually for a periodic payment called rent. According to Olawoyin, tenancy implies a contractual relationship where exclusive possession is granted for a specific term in exchange for consideration.⁴ Similarly, Agbede describes tenancy as a temporary interest in land granted by the landlord to another person for use and enjoyment, subject to agreed terms.⁵ In the view of Onifade, tenancy is both a contractual and possessory interest, often determined by the duration and intention of the parties.⁶ Adefi expands this by stating that tenancy relationships may exist even without formal written agreements, provided possession is granted and rent is accepted.⁷ Ogundele emphasized the exclusive right of the tenant to occupy the premises as a critical legal element that distinguishes tenancy from mere license.⁸ Nnamani viewed tenancy as involving legal obligations and duties that are enforceable by both parties under the relevant tenancy statutes.³ Similarly, Ajayi argued that tenancy can only be established where there is clear intention to create a legal relationship, evidenced by possession, payment, and mutual agreement.⁹

From the above, tenancy can be operationally defined as a legally recognized contractual arrangement between a landlord and a tenant, where the tenant enjoys exclusive possession of property for a period of time in exchange for periodic rent, whether the terms are expressly written or implied by conduct. This definition is central to understanding tenancy disputes, as most conflicts arise from disagreements over the rights, duration, and obligations embedded in such agreements. Tenancies in Nigeria can be classified into various types, depending on the duration and nature of the rental arrangement. These include weekly tenancy, monthly tenancy, yearly tenancy, tenancy at will, and statutory tenancy. According to Mbah, a weekly tenancy involves an agreement where rent is paid weekly and is typically terminable by a week's notice.¹⁰ Monthly tenancy, on the other hand, involves monthly rental payments and is common in urban residential accommodations.¹¹ A yearly tenancy, as observed by Iwuoha, involves a longer-term rental arrangement with more formalized agreements and typically requires a six-month notice before termination.¹² Tenancy at will refers to a flexible arrangement that can be terminated at any time by either party, usually without formal notice, as described by Chijioke.¹³ A statutory tenancy arises where a tenant remains in possession after the expiration of the tenancy and is protected by statute from summary eviction.¹⁴ In all these variations, the existence and duration of a tenancy are key factors in determining rights and remedies. Thus, a sound conceptual understanding of tenancy and its types forms the foundation for assessing legal disputes arising from landlord-tenant relationships. This leads logically to the legal framework governing tenancy relations in Nigeria, which includes national statutes, state tenancy laws, and judicial interpretations, that provide the legal context for resolving tenancy disputes.

## Rent Control Laws and Landlord-Tenant Roles in Nigeria

The legal architecture regulating tenancy relationships in Nigeria is multifaceted, comprising both federal and state-level statutes. A central instrument in this framework is the Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Act, alongside various tenancy laws enacted by state governments to address local housing dynamics. These laws provide clarity on rent regulation, eviction procedures, and rights of occupation, and dispute resolution, all of which play pivotal roles in shaping the landlord-tenant relationship in Nigeria's property landscape.

The Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Act was originally enacted during the military era and applied to select urban centers. Although not uniformly enforced across all states, it laid the groundwork for legal safeguards that protect tenants against arbitrary eviction and exploitative rent practices. The Act prescribes procedures that must be strictly followed by landlords seeking to recover possession of premises, including the service of statutory notices, proper filing of court processes, and obtaining possession orders from the magistrate or high courts. This legislative framework aims to strike a balance between the proprietary rights of landlords and the occupational rights of tenants.¹⁵ In practice, the application of the Act has been localized. States such as Lagos, Rivers, Enugu, and the Federal Capital Territory have enacted their own tenancy laws to reflect local socio-economic conditions. The Lagos State Tenancy Law of 2011, for example, has emerged as a comprehensive statute that not only regulates rent increments and recovery procedures but also outlaws the practice of collecting more than one year's rent in advance for residential premises. The law also introduces alternative dispute resolution mechanisms such as mediation and arbitration to promote quicker resolution of tenancy matters without resorting to lengthy litigation.¹⁶

Similarly, the Federal Capital Territory Tenancy Law reinforces the requirement of a quit notice and ensures that rent increases are not retroactive but must comply with agreed contractual terms. These developments highlight the decentralized nature of tenancy law in Nigeria and the emphasis placed on tenant protection across jurisdictions.

Beyond legislative texts, the practical enforcement of tenancy law revolves around the clearly defined rights and duties of both landlords and tenants. Landlords, as property owners, are entitled to receive rent, recover possession upon proper notice, and have their property maintained by the tenant. However, these rights are counterbalanced by a legal obligation to maintain the premises in habitable condition, respect the terms of the lease, and refrain from using unlawful force or intimidation to evict tenants.¹⁷ A landlord who breaches these duties risks both civil liability and criminal sanctions under extant laws. Tenants, on the other hand, enjoy the right to peaceful and exclusive occupation of the premises for the duration of the tenancy. They are also protected against sudden rent hikes or forceful eviction without due process. Nevertheless, tenants are expected to fulfill their responsibilities, which include timely payment of rent, proper use of the premises, and compliance with tenancy terms. Where a tenant defaults, the law empowers the landlord to seek judicial remedies through formal notice and court-sanctioned repossession.¹⁸ The understanding of these roles is not merely theoretical but has practical implications in managing the high incidence of tenancy disputes in Nigeria. Disputes often arise from unilateral eviction attempts, disputes over rent arrears, or breaches of maintenance responsibilities. In these cases, courts are guided by the statutory frameworks and evidence of compliance with procedural requirements. The increasing reliance on state tenancy laws has also led to the emergence of specialized tribunals and informal dispute mechanisms that attempt to resolve issues without burdening the formal judicial system.¹⁹ Eventually, the evolving nature of tenancy legislation in Nigeria underscores the importance of legal clarity, procedural compliance, and awareness of rights and responsibilities by both landlords and tenants. This legislative and operational framework forms the basis for evaluating judicial decisions and enforcement challenges that will be addressed in subsequent sections.

## Common Causes of Tenancy Disputes in Nigeria

Tenancy disputes remain one of the most contentious areas of property law in Nigeria, primarily due to the complex interaction between customary practices, statutory provisions, and economic realities. These disputes often arise from either party failing to adhere to the terms and conditions of tenancy agreements, a lack of awareness of legal obligations, or deliberate acts of bad faith. The effects are far-reaching, disrupting housing stability, straining judicial resources, and undermining the peaceful enjoyment of property. The following eight issues have been identified as the most frequent causes of tenancy disputes in Nigerian residential and commercial property contexts:

### Failure to Pay Rent

One of the most common causes of landlord-tenant conflict is the tenant's failure to pay rent as and when due. In Nigeria, tenancy agreements usually provide for rent to be paid monthly, quarterly, or annually. However, due to financial constraints, economic instability, or deliberate noncompliance, tenants sometimes default on their obligations. Landlords who attempt to evict such tenants without complying with due legal process often find themselves embroiled in disputes. The law mandates specific procedures for eviction, and any deviation from these procedures creates grounds for litigation.²⁰

### Illegal or Forceful Eviction

Illegal eviction, also known as self-help eviction, occurs when landlords use physical force or coercive tactics to remove tenants without obtaining a court order. These actions include padlocking entrances, removing roofs or doors, or disconnecting water and electricity supplies. Nigerian courts have consistently held that such acts violate the fundamental rights of tenants and amount to unlawful eviction, even when the tenant is in rent arrears.²¹ Disputes of this nature are frequent and usually result in damages being awarded against the landlord.

### Disagreements on Tenancy Terms

In many instances, tenancy agreements are not documented, or where written, they lack clarity and specificity. Ambiguities in rental duration, renewal terms, utility responsibilities, or occupancy limits often result in conflicting interpretations between landlords and tenants. Such disagreements become legal issues when one party claims breach of agreement or wrongful termination. Legal scholars have emphasized that clear, detailed, and legally binding tenancy agreements reduce the incidence of such disputes.²²

### Breach of Covenant by Tenant

Covenants are specific promises within a tenancy agreement, such as restrictions against subletting, making alterations to the property, or engaging in unlawful activities on the premises. When tenants breach any of these conditions, landlords may seek possession of the premises. However, disputes arise when landlords fail to provide appropriate notice, or when the breach is contested by the tenant. For instance, a tenant who makes renovations without consent may argue necessity, while the landlord may allege trespass.²³

### Failure to Issue or Accept Valid Notice to Quit

Under Nigerian tenancy law, the issuance of a proper notice to quit is a statutory requirement before a landlord can lawfully recover possession of premises. The length and type of notice depend on the tenancy structure — weekly, monthly, or yearly. Landlords who fail to issue notices in compliance with the law risk having their cases struck out, even where other grounds for eviction exist. Conversely, tenants may refuse to vacate, arguing that the notice given was invalid or improperly served.²⁴ This procedural error often escalates into full-scale litigation.

### Unlawful Increase of Rent

Another common cause of conflict is the arbitrary or unilateral increase in rent by landlords without adequate notice or justification. In many urban centers, especially in Lagos and Abuja, where demand for housing exceeds supply, landlords exploit tenants by suddenly increasing rent. In the absence of rent control laws or enforceable tenancy regulations, tenants frequently challenge such increases in court, particularly where they are not supported by improvements in the property.²⁵

### Failure of Landlord to Maintain the Property

Landlords have a legal obligation to ensure that the premises leased are habitable and fit for human occupation. This includes responsibilities such as plumbing repairs, structural maintenance, and pest control. When landlords neglect these duties, tenants may withhold rent or seek redress, resulting in disputes. In some cases, landlords dispute the scope of their obligations, especially where agreements are silent or vague on maintenance terms.²⁶ The absence of regular inspections and formal channels for complaints exacerbates these tensions.

### Refusal to Refund Security Deposit

At the end of a tenancy, tenants expect landlords to return their security deposits, provided there is no damage to the property beyond normal wear and tear. However, many landlords refuse to refund these deposits, alleging property damage or unpaid bills without providing substantiating evidence. In some cases, the initial deposit was undocumented, or no inventory was taken, making it difficult to establish whether deductions are legitimate. Such practices contribute significantly to the volume of post-tenancy disputes.²⁷

Hence, tenancy disputes in Nigeria typically arise from breaches of tenancy obligations, procedural errors in eviction, and lack of clarity in rental arrangements. These issues reflect broader challenges in housing regulation, legal literacy, and enforcement of statutory protections. Addressing them requires not only legal reform but also enhanced public education and dispute resolution mechanisms.

## Legal Remedies Available to Parties in Tenancy Disputes

In the Nigerian property law system, the resolution of tenancy disputes is grounded in a mix of statutory provisions, common law principles, and increasingly, alternative dispute resolution (ADR) frameworks. These remedies provide both landlords and tenants with avenues to seek redress when tenancy obligations are breached. The effectiveness of each remedy depends on the nature of the dispute, the applicable law, and the forum in which the matter is heard. The remedies fall into four broad categories as discussed below:

### Statutory Remedies under Recovery of Premises Laws

The primary statutory remedy available to landlords in Nigeria is the recovery of possession through the Recovery of Premises Act (for the Federal Capital Territory) or the Tenancy Laws of respective states. These laws prescribe the process for lawful eviction, including the issuance of notice to quit and notice of owner's intention to recover possession. Upon the expiration of these notices, landlords may approach a competent court to obtain an order for possession. The statutes are designed to prevent illegal eviction and ensure that tenants are not forcefully ejected without judicial oversight.²⁸ Tenants may also rely on these laws to defend against unlawful ejection and demand compliance with statutory procedures.

### Common Law Remedies

At common law, several remedies are available to either party in a tenancy relationship. One such remedy is specific performance, which may be ordered by a court compelling a party to fulfil a contractual obligation, such as reinstating a tenant unlawfully evicted before the expiration of the tenancy. Damages may also be awarded as compensation for losses suffered due to breach of the tenancy agreement, such as failure to maintain premises or wrongful eviction. Injunctions can be issued to restrain landlords from taking illegal actions, such as disconnecting utilities or entering the premises without permission.²¹ These remedies reinforce the contractual nature of tenancy relationships and emphasize the protection of rights through judicial enforcement.

### Court Jurisdiction: Magistrate versus High Courts

The question of jurisdiction in tenancy matters depends on the value of the premises and the nature of relief sought. Most tenancy disputes fall within the jurisdiction of Magistrate Courts, particularly those involving recovery of possession and arrears of rent within the monetary threshold. However, when the dispute involves substantial claims for damages or raises constitutional issues such as breach of fundamental rights, the High Court becomes the appropriate forum.²⁹ This distinction is critical, as filing a tenancy suit in the wrong court can lead to dismissal for lack of jurisdiction.

### Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) Mechanisms

Increasingly, tenancy disputes in Nigeria are being resolved through ADR mechanisms such as mediation, arbitration, and customary resolution systems. Mediation allows parties to resolve issues amicably with the assistance of a neutral third party, often saving time and preserving relationships. Arbitration is less common in residential tenancy matters but may be included as a clause in commercial tenancy agreements. Customary resolution methods, often involving community elders or local chiefs, are widely used in rural areas where statutory legal processes are seen as expensive or inaccessible.³⁰ These non-judicial methods provide accessible and culturally sensitive means of resolving disputes, although their enforceability may be limited if not documented or recognized by formal courts.

Hence, the legal remedies available to parties in tenancy disputes reflect a pluralistic legal framework combining statutory law, common law principles, and traditional forms of justice. For these remedies to be effective, both landlords and tenants must be aware of their rights and obligations, while legal institutions must ensure timely and fair enforcement.

## Review of Judicial Decisions on Tenancy Disputes in Nigeria

Judicial decisions have played a crucial role in shaping the interpretation and enforcement of tenancy laws in Nigeria. Courts have often clarified ambiguous statutory provisions, developed equitable remedies, and addressed procedural lapses in landlord-tenant relationships. A careful review of key judicial pronouncements offers insights into how the Nigerian judiciary approaches tenancy disputes, particularly in terms of statutory interpretation, procedural compliance, and tenant protection.

### Analysis of Key Nigerian Cases

The Nigerian courts have consistently decided landmark tenancy cases that have become judicial precedents. In *Ayinke Stores Ltd v. Adebogun* (2008), the Supreme Court emphasized that recovery of premises must strictly comply with the procedural requirements under the Recovery of Premises Act. The court held that even where a tenant was in default, failure to issue proper statutory notices rendered the landlord's action invalid. Similarly, in *Ihenacho v. Uzochukwu* (1997), the court reiterated that resorting to self-help by landlords was unlawful, and tenants were entitled to remedies where evicted without due process. Another instructive case is *Ibiyemi v. Bamidele* (1967), where the court ruled that service of quit notice must be in strict compliance with the tenancy agreement and the relevant laws, failing which the action for possession would be premature.

### Trends in Judicial Reasoning

A recurring trend in judicial reasoning is the protection of the weaker party, often the tenant, in disputes where landlords are seen to exercise excessive power. The courts have adopted a purposive approach in interpreting tenancy laws, ensuring that technicalities do not defeat the intent of the legislature. There is also increasing judicial sensitivity to social justice, especially in urban eviction cases, where courts have urged landlords to adopt lawful means of recovery and discouraged arbitrary displacements of tenants.³¹

### Statutory Interpretation by the Courts

Nigerian courts have often been called upon to interpret provisions of the Recovery of Premises Act and Tenancy Laws of various states. Courts have clarified the legal meanings of "tenant," "landlord," and "premises" to establish jurisdiction and determine whether proper notices were served. For instance, in *Coker v. Adesanya* (1976), the court held that a notice to quit must indicate the correct duration of the tenancy and align with the contractual terms agreed upon by both parties. Judicial interpretation has also extended to the computation of notice periods, distinctions between fixed and periodic tenancies, and the legal consequences of holding over after the expiration of tenancy.

### Procedural Technicalities and Their Impact on Justice

While courts have emphasized strict compliance with procedural rules, there is growing concern that excessive technicality sometimes impedes substantive justice. Landlords who fail to follow procedural steps meticulously may lose possession claims, even when tenants are clearly in breach. Conversely, tenants who lack legal representation may be evicted unjustly due to ignorance of procedural rights. The courts have sought to balance these outcomes by emphasizing access to justice, fair hearing, and procedural equity.³² However, inconsistencies still exist in the application of these standards, leading to calls for reform and simplification of tenancy procedures.

Consequently, judicial decisions in tenancy matters demonstrate the dynamic interplay between statutory rules, contractual obligations, and equitable principles. The Nigerian judiciary has consistently upheld tenants' rights while reinforcing the importance of lawful procedures in property disputes. These judgments contribute to the development of a predictable legal environment for both landlords and tenants and serve as guideposts for legal practitioners and policymakers.

## Enforcement Challenges in Tenancy Disputes in Nigeria

Despite the existence of a robust legal framework for tenancy relationships in Nigeria, the enforcement of tenancy rights and remedies continues to face significant obstacles. These challenges affect both landlords and tenants and often undermine the objective of achieving justice and fairness in landlord-tenant relations.

### Delays in Judicial Processes

One of the most pressing enforcement challenges in tenancy disputes is the delay experienced in Nigerian courts. Tenancy matters, which ideally should be disposed of speedily due to their socio-economic implications, often take several months or years before judgment is delivered. These delays are caused by factors such as court congestion, frequent adjournments, and insufficient judicial manpower. The delay in adjudication denies landlords prompt recovery of premises and subjects tenants to prolonged uncertainty. Judicial delays in tenancy disputes erode public confidence in the legal system and often encourage resort to self-help.³²

### Abuse of Court Processes by Landlords or Tenants

Abuse of legal procedures by both landlords and tenants is another common challenge. Landlords may file frivolous suits to intimidate tenants, while tenants may deliberately frustrate proceedings by challenging jurisdiction or seeking repeated adjournments. In many instances, parties exploit technicalities or loopholes in the legal system to gain undue advantage. For example, some landlords initiate eviction proceedings without proper notices, knowing that litigation costs and delays may discourage tenants from contesting. Conversely, some tenants remain in possession long after the expiration of their tenancy by invoking baseless defences or filing counterclaims.³³

### Lack of Awareness of Legal Rights by Tenants

A significant portion of tenants in Nigeria, particularly in rural and low-income urban areas, are unaware of their legal rights. This ignorance often leads to their exploitation by unscrupulous landlords who engage in unlawful evictions or impose unreasonable tenancy terms. Many tenants do not know that they are entitled to quit notices and legal redress in the event of breach. This knowledge gap contributes to the underreporting of tenancy abuses and the ineffectiveness of legal remedies. Nwogugu emphasized that legal literacy among tenants is essential for the enforcement of housing rights.²²

### Corruption and Inefficiency in Law Enforcement

Corruption within the law enforcement agencies and the judiciary has also been identified as a major hindrance to effective enforcement of tenancy laws. There are reported cases where court bailiffs or police officers demand unofficial payments before executing lawful eviction orders. Similarly, some judicial officers compromise due process, resulting in biased decisions or prolonged proceedings. This undermines public trust in the legal system and perpetuates impunity. According to Elias, corruption distorts justice delivery and frustrates the realization of tenancy protections enshrined in law.²⁹

### Inadequate Implementation of Tenancy Laws by Local Authorities

The enforcement of tenancy legislation often falls under the purview of local government authorities, yet many of them lack the institutional capacity, resources, and willpower to effectively implement these laws. Some states have tenancy tribunals or rent control boards, but these institutions are either moribund or non-functional due to poor funding or political interference. As a result, tenancy laws remain on paper with little or no practical impact. Omotola noted that the absence of functional regulatory frameworks at the grassroots weakens the legal protection of tenants and emboldens erring landlords.³³

Nevertheless, the effective enforcement of tenancy rights in Nigeria is hindered by a combination of systemic inefficiencies, procedural abuse, ignorance of legal rights, and institutional corruption. Addressing these challenges requires judicial reforms, legal education, and strengthened regulatory oversight by local and state governments.

## Policy and Legal Implications in Tenancy Dispute Management in Nigeria

The regulatory environment surrounding landlord and tenant relationships in Nigeria is significantly shaped by a mix of statutory and customary provisions. However, the increasing volume of tenancy disputes and the inefficiency in their resolution suggest that deeper policy and legal reforms are required to ensure equitable and consistent enforcement of tenancy rights.

### Weaknesses in Existing Laws

One of the primary policy concerns relates to the inadequacies of existing tenancy legislation across Nigeria. While the Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Act provides a federal template, tenancy laws are largely domesticated and implemented by states, many of which have outdated statutes that do not reflect contemporary housing realities. These laws often lack clear procedures for eviction, are silent on issues such as digital tenancy agreements, and do not provide adequate protections against unlawful rent hikes or eviction without notice. According to Nwafor, outdated tenancy laws have contributed to legal ambiguities and have made it difficult for courts to adjudicate disputes uniformly.¹⁴

### Need for Harmonization of Tenancy Laws Across States

Another critical policy implication is the absence of harmonized tenancy legislation across the country. The current system allows each state to legislate independently on tenancy matters, leading to inconsistency in interpretation and enforcement. In some states, for instance, the notice period for eviction is two weeks, while others require one month or more. This disparity creates legal uncertainty, especially for landlords or tenants who move between jurisdictions. Harmonization of tenancy statutes across states, possibly through a uniform Tenancy Code or a model law adopted nationally, has been recommended to ensure clarity and legal predictability.¹⁶

### Calls for Reform in Landlord-Tenant Relationship Regulation

There is a growing consensus among legal scholars and housing rights advocates on the need for a more equitable framework regulating landlord-tenant relationships. Reforms should prioritize fairness, transparency, and accountability in rental agreements. Legislative efforts should also target the curbing of arbitrary rent increases, provision of rent tribunals for swift redress, and guidelines on minimum living conditions. Okpaluba argued that reforms in tenancy law should be both people-centered and responsive to emerging housing dynamics such as short-term leases, co-tenancy, and urban slum tenancy practices.²⁵

### Role of Legal Aid and Tenants' Advocacy

Another major policy concern is the limited access to legal representation among tenants, particularly those in low-income brackets. Many tenants who suffer unjust evictions or exploitation are unable to pursue legal remedies due to financial constraints or lack of legal knowledge. Legal aid schemes and tenant advocacy groups play a vital role in bridging this gap by offering free legal advice, public sensitization, and litigation support. Strengthening and expanding these support systems can help ensure that vulnerable tenants are protected and that the landlord-tenant relationship operates within the bounds of fairness and legality. As noted by Onuoha, investment in legal aid infrastructure and community-based legal awareness can significantly reduce tenancy-related injustices in Nigeria.²⁷

For this reason, the policy and legal implications of tenancy dispute resolution in Nigeria call for a comprehensive review and modernization of tenancy laws, standardization of legal provisions across states, stronger institutional frameworks for tenant protection, and enhanced access to legal aid services.

## Conclusion

Tenancy disputes in Nigeria remain a recurring legal and social concern, stemming from a combination of outdated statutory provisions, inconsistent judicial interpretation, and weak enforcement structures. The relationship between landlords and tenants is often marked by mutual suspicion, frequent breaches of obligations, and a lack of awareness of legal rights and duties. Despite the existence of laws such as the Rent Control and Recovery of Premises Acts across various states, their fragmented application has not guaranteed effective protection for either party. Importantly, judicial decisions on tenancy matters, while extensive, have not always provided the clarity or uniformity required to guide both litigants and practitioners. The overlapping jurisdiction between courts and the procedural hurdles tenants often face contribute to a system where legal redress is slow, expensive, and at times, inaccessible. These challenges have encouraged informal or forceful approaches to eviction and rent recovery, weakening public confidence in the rule of law. Thus, it is essential to critically re-evaluate the legal and institutional frameworks governing tenancy in Nigeria. Strengthening enforcement mechanisms, ensuring legal clarity, and enhancing access to justice would go a long way in promoting fairness, order, and mutual respect in landlord-tenant relationships.

---

## Suggestions

In light of the issues identified, several practical recommendations are proposed to address the challenges associated with tenancy disputes in Nigeria:

1. **Harmonization of Tenancy Laws:** There is a critical need to standardize tenancy laws across states to reduce inconsistencies and facilitate uniform judicial interpretation. A national tenancy code could serve as a model law adaptable by all states.

2. **Establishment of Specialized Housing Tribunals:** The creation of dedicated housing courts or tribunals with simplified procedures and trained adjudicators would expedite the resolution of tenancy disputes and decongest existing courts.

3. **Public Legal Education:** Targeted public education campaigns should be initiated by legal aid councils, housing authorities, and civil society organizations to raise awareness among tenants and landlords about their rights and responsibilities.

4. **Expansion of Legal Aid Services:** Strengthening and extending legal aid services to cover tenancy disputes — especially for vulnerable tenants — would promote equitable access to justice and reduce exploitation.

5. **Promotion of Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR):** Encouraging the use of ADR mechanisms such as mediation and arbitration in tenancy conflicts can help de-escalate disputes quickly and reduce the burden on the judiciary.

6. **Digitization and Documentation of Tenancy Agreements:** Efforts should be made to ensure that tenancy agreements are properly documented, digitized, and registered to minimize evidentiary disputes in court.

7. **Enforcement Reforms:** Law enforcement agents tasked with executing court orders in tenancy matters should be better trained and monitored to eliminate corruption and abuse of power.
